TBM 425: AI and Agency

Cutler argues that AI adoption mandates often destroy the agency they claim to promote: teams need room to test, reject, and adapt tools against real problems, not perform adoption for executive metrics.

  • AI adoption is not just a training problem; trust, autonomy, and local context determine whether people use the tools well.
  • Skepticism is part of agency, especially when teams are being asked to change craft practices under management pressure.
  • Useful AI rollout starts from real work and evidence, not quota-style proof that people are prompting enough.

How's Linear so fast? A technical breakdown

Brotzky breaks down Linear's perceived speed as an architecture choice: IndexedDB-backed local state, optimistic mutations, background sync, small modern-browser bundles, precaching, and animations that avoid blocking the workflow.

  • The browser-side database removes network latency from the core interaction loop; sync becomes a background concern.
  • Fast product feel is cumulative: local-first data, chunking, service-worker precache, GPU-friendly animation, and keyboard-first flows reinforce each other.
  • The tradeoff is architectural commitment; Linear started with a sync engine because that foundation is hard to retrofit later.

Reverse engineering the Creative Katana V2X soundbar to be able to control it from Linux

The write-up documents a disciplined hardware/protocol reverse-engineering path: inspect the vendor app, capture USB traffic operation by operation, infer the serial framing, and build Linux control tooling from reproducible packet evidence.

  • Separate captures per UI operation turn opaque proprietary behavior into a tractable protocol map.
  • Firmware update traffic can expose payload format and command structure when ordinary controls are still unclear.
  • The method matters more than the soundbar: isolate the transport, record known actions, infer framing, then implement the minimum useful control surface.

The thread surfaces a practical middle position on AI in engineering: even skeptics can use models as cheap review assistants for obvious mistakes, leakage, bad payload paths, and ignored error logs.

  • The useful baseline is not autonomous coding; it is catching defects a careful reviewer would catch when humans are moving too fast.
  • AI review adds value when paired with engineering judgment and concrete diffs, not when treated as authority.
  • The examples point to process failure: teams that will not inspect logs or test assumptions need stronger review habits, with or without AI.

Allowing LLM's to work fully autonomously is only viable when you have a process that automatically verifies it

The post argues from a legacy-porting case that agentic coding only becomes viable when every change is constrained by an independent verifier, such as deterministic output diffs between old and new systems.

  • The hard part of AI coding is not generating code; it is proving that behavior stayed correct.
  • A good autonomy loop gives the model a task, runs it against a verifier, feeds back the concrete mismatch, and repeats.
  • Verification has to be external to the model's own explanation, especially for legacy migrations where plausible code can still be wrong.
